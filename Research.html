BEAST

Complementing indirect detection techniques, direct imaging is starting to probe the outskirts of young planetary systems, where giant planet formation occurs. Through my work within the BEAST survey, I am trying to shed light on planet formation around high-mass stars.

OBTAINING INDIRECT SYSTEM AGES

Young stars, the best target for direct imaging surveys, are often found in stellar associations. I exploit kinematic information provided by Gaia to indirectly constrain the age of individual systems through their kinematic neighbors.

MADYS

A fast tool for isochronal age determination of groups of stars. MADYS automatically collects photometry from different catalogs, corrects for extinction, and compares photometry with 12 theoretical isochrone grids.

SUBSTRUCTURES IN UPPER SCORPIUS

Combining my kinematic approach and the strength of MADYS, I was able to identify a network of kinematic structures inside the Upper Scorpius association. Non-clustered stars are older than their clustered counterpart.

BEAST

Pushing the frontier of exoplanet surveys

Most of the past and ongoing exoplanet surveys have focused on stars as massive as or less massive than the Sun, and about 90% of the ∼4500 known exoplanets lie closer to their stars than the Earth is to the Sun. What lurks in the outskirts of high-mass stars systems, waiting to be discovered? This is the scientific question that the B-star Exoplanet Abundance Study (BEAST) is trying to answer.

Into the unknown

With just one third of the targets observed twice, BEAST has already started to give exciting results. We have recently presented the discovery of a giant planet with 10.9±1.6 M_J around the binary b Cen (Janson et al., Nature 600, 231), and I'm leading a second paper on an even more exciting scientific case (Squicciarini et al., Science, submitted). How can the existence of these planets be reconciled with the current models of planetary formation?

Companion characterization

What is the frequency of giant planets and brown dwarfs around B stars? What is the formation model that best explains the observations? Every new discovery is a piece of a huge jigsaw puzzle, whose complete picture we are trying to grasp. But to have each piece in the right spot, precise estimate of both stellar properties (mass, age) and companion properties (mass, orbital parameters) are required. To this end, I have combined kinematic information from Gaia and a new age determination tool, MADYS.

OBTAINING INDIRECT SYSTEM AGES

Never ask a B star its age

Precise system ages are mandatory both for a meaningful comparison of direct imaging observations with model predictions and for demographic purposes. However, due to a combination of factors (distance uncertainty, rapid rotation, magnetic fields, unresolved multiplicity, luminosity almost independent of age after 2-3 Myr), direct age determinations for B stars are extremely difficult. Tackling this problem has been a major part of my involvement within BEAST.

Identifying kinematic subgroups

Using Gaia parallaxes and proper motions, I identified comoving stars (CMS), i.e. stars with similar 2D-projected space motions, to most BEAST targets. The identified groups can be seen as co-moving streams sprouting from a common extended star formation event, giving the opportunity to indirectly constrain the age of the target star. Individual isochronal ages for each CMS are computed using MADYS.

MADYS

Dating stars

The renewed interest in young clusters and associations thanks to Gaia, on the one hand, and the flourishing of planet-hunting direct imaging surveys, on the other hand, highlight the need for a uniform and consistent derivation of stellar parameters (age, mass) for large groups of stars. To this end, I have developed MADYS, a flexible Python tool for age and mass determination of young stellar and substellar objects based on the comparison between photometric data and theoretical isochrone grids.

learn more

A mock CMD from MADYS.

Squicciarini et al. (2021)

Automation is the way

Given a list of stars, MADYS automatically retrieves and cross-matches photometry from different catalogues, corrects for interstellar extinction, and employs reliable photometric data to rapidly derive ages and masses of individual stars. Harmonizing the heterogeneity of publicly-available isochrone grids, MADYS allows to select one among 13 models, many of which offering the possibility to customize metallicity, rotational velocity and other astrophysical parameters, for a total of ~80 different isochrone grids.

learn more

G-band absorption in the direction of Upper Scorpius.

Janson et al. (2021a)

Multiple applications

The versatility of MADYS is one among its chief strengths: from indirect age determinations (Janson+21) to direct mass estimates (Bonavita+21), from the computation of detection map for direct imaging surveys to a thorough age comparison of different populations (Squicciarini+21), and even to the selection of the best targets for future surveys (Ray et al. in prep), MADYS has the potential to become highly beneficial to the whole community.

SUBSTRUCTURES IN UPPER SCORPIUS

Trace-back analysis

Members of s​tellar associations can be distinguished from field interlopers because of a common average proper motion. This common motion, gradually eroded by the galactic tidal field over time, is in turn reminiscent of the initial kinematic structure. Combining astrometry and kinematics from Gaia EDR3, and radial velocities from GALAH and APOGEE, I traced back the present positions of stars belonging to Upper Scorpius (USCO), a subgroup of Scorpius–Centaurus, the nearest OB association to the Sun.
